« The heart is the beginning of life; the sun of the microcosm. »William Harvey, De Motu Cordis, 1628
« The landscape drawn so far, from macrophages to T lymphocytes, from natural killer cells to dendritic cells and mast cells, remained that of hematopoietic cells. Yet the thesis of the heart as an immune organ is fulfilled only by recognizing that its non-hematopoietic resident cells, long reduced to contractile, barrier or structural roles, themselves take part in danger sensing and in the orchestration of inflammation. The cardiomyocyte, the endothelium and the fibroblast possess the receptor repertoire, the signalling pathways and the secretory capacities of true sentinels; their engagement sets the magnitude, the location and the resolution of the myocardial immune response. »Manifesto, §2.7
« This is the mechanistic node that links seemingly heterogeneous cardiovascular insults: cholesterol and urate crystals, once phagocytosed, rupture the lysosomal membrane and activate the inflammasome, while hyperglycaemia and the danger signals released by ischaemia converge on the same platform. In myocardial infarction, the inflammasome is activated at reperfusion and governs, through the interleukins, adverse ventricular remodelling, cytokine-driven systolic dysfunction and the progression toward heart failure. This biology underlies the anti-interleukin revolution developed further on. »Manifesto, §3.4
« The resolution of inflammation, long overlooked, is not a passive exhaustion of pro-inflammatory signals but an active, programmed process, governed by specialised pro-resolving lipid mediators: lipoxins derived from arachidonic acid, resolvins arising from the omega-3 fatty acids, protectins and maresins. These mediators curb the recruitment of neutrophils, promote the clearance of apoptotic cells and reprogram macrophages toward a reparative phenotype. The failure of this active resolution, more than an excess of initial inflammation, underlies the chronicity of immune-mediated heart disease. »Manifesto, §4.4
